# MockMate AI Design Language

## 1. Brand Personality

MockMate AI should feel like a high-performance coaching studio, not a playful AI toy.

### Emotions Users Should Feel

- Grounded confidence: "I can improve with this."
- Psychological safety: "I am being coached, not judged."
- Momentum: "I know my next step."
- Professional pride: "This feels like a serious platform used by serious people."
- Trust: "My data and progress are handled responsibly."

### What Recruiters or Decision Makers Should Think

- "This product understands real interview quality, not just flashy AI output."
- "The brand feels premium, structured, and reliable enough for institutional use."
- "The design communicates credibility and execution maturity."
- "This platform can represent our organization without looking generic."

## 2. Design Principles (Max 5)

1. Clarity over novelty
Use visual design to reduce anxiety and improve comprehension at every step.

2. Human coaching over AI spectacle
Frame AI as assistive intelligence. The user remains the protagonist.

3. Precision over decoration
Every color, spacing choice, and motion cue should communicate hierarchy or feedback.

4. Calm premium energy
Feel sophisticated and modern without dark, neon, or trend-driven gimmicks.

5. System-first consistency
From landing pages to dashboards, patterns should feel unified and intentional.

## 3. Color System

Avoiding generic blue-purple AI palettes, this system uses graphite, deep forest, warm amber, and porcelain neutrals to feel distinct, premium, and trustworthy.

- Primary: #1E3A34
- Secondary: #7A5C3E
- Accent: #C07B2A
- Background: #F6F4F0
- Surface: #FFFCF7
- Border: #D8D2C8
- Success: #2E7D4F
- Warning: #B7791F
- Error: #B5413C

### Palette Intent

- Primary anchors authority and focus without default SaaS blue.
- Secondary introduces editorial warmth and human coaching tone.
- Accent is used sparingly for high-intent moments and premium highlights.
- Background and surface keep interfaces bright, tactile, and calm.
- Semantic colors are vivid enough for status clarity without oversaturation.

## 4. Typography (Google Fonts)

### Recommended Pairing

- Headline and Display: Sora
- Body and Interface: Inter
- Optional Data and Numeric UI: IBM Plex Sans

### Why This Works

- Sora gives a modern, geometric voice that feels advanced but still approachable.
- Inter is highly legible for dense product interfaces and long dashboard sessions.
- IBM Plex Sans can strengthen data-heavy areas where numerics and tabular reading matter.

### Tone Strategy

- Sora for brand presence, campaign headlines, and top-level statements.
- Inter for everything operational: forms, cards, dashboards, and explanatory copy.
- Keep hierarchy weight-driven before relying on color or visual effects.

## 5. Border Radius

Rounded but disciplined, never bubbly.

- XS: 6px
- SM: 10px
- MD: 14px
- LG: 20px
- XL: 28px

Usage:

- Inputs and compact controls: XS to SM
- Cards and panels: SM to MD
- Hero media frames and featured surfaces: LG
- Avoid full-pill corners except badges or chips.

## 6. Shadows

Use layered, low-contrast shadows for depth without visual noise.

- Shadow 1 (Resting): 0 1px 2px rgba(25, 21, 17, 0.06)
- Shadow 2 (Elevated): 0 8px 24px rgba(25, 21, 17, 0.10)
- Shadow 3 (Floating): 0 18px 48px rgba(25, 21, 17, 0.14)

Guidance:

- Prefer borders plus subtle shadow over heavy drop shadows.
- Elevate only interactive or prioritized surfaces.
- Maintain consistent light direction and softness.

## 7. Spacing Scale

Use a 4px base and a practical scale for both marketing and app UI.

- 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 32, 40, 48, 64, 80, 96

Guidance:

- Vertical rhythm should be generous in marketing surfaces and efficient in dashboards.
- Use consistent spacing jumps to improve perceived system quality.
- Never compress critical content blocks below readable comfort.

## 8. Icon Style

- Stroke-based icons with moderate corner softening.
- Visual weight should be consistent across sizes.
- Avoid cartoonish metaphor icons.
- Use icon color as supportive hierarchy, not primary meaning.

Direction:

- Clear, technical, human-centered symbols.
- Product states should read instantly at small sizes.

## 9. Illustration Style

No stock photography. No generic AI head silhouettes. No glowing brain graphics.

Preferred style:

- Product-native scene illustrations built from UI fragments.
- Abstract editorial geometry inspired by interview flow and progression.
- Soft grain, subtle gradients, and layered cards for depth.
- Human presence implied through behavior and outcomes, not literal photos.

Narrative cues to visualize:

- Preparation to performance journey.
- Signal clarity and confidence growth.
- Professional coaching and measurable progress.

## 10. Animation Philosophy

Motion should reinforce confidence, hierarchy, and causality.

- Use fades, subtle slide, and micro-scale transitions.
- Keep durations mostly between 160ms and 320ms.
- Use slower transitions only for major scene changes.
- Respect reduced-motion preferences by default.

Rule:

If animation does not improve clarity, remove it.

## 11. Button Philosophy

Buttons should communicate action confidence and decision hierarchy.

- One dominant primary CTA per surface.
- Secondary CTAs should never visually compete with the primary action.
- Destructive actions should feel deliberate and controlled.
- Hover and press states should be tactile but restrained.

Visual character:

- Strong shape, refined typography, purposeful contrast.
- No glossy gradients or gamified micro-effects.

## 12. Card Philosophy

Cards are containers of meaning, not decorative tiles.

- Every card must answer one question quickly.
- Prefer compact, scannable structure: title, key metric, context, next action.
- Separate groups by content logic, not by arbitrary color changes.
- Use spacing and typography hierarchy before adding borders or shadows.

## 13. Dashboard Philosophy

Dashboards should feel like a control room for progress, not a noisy report wall.

- Prioritize current state, next actions, and trend direction.
- Keep data density role-aware for students, teachers, and admins.
- Use visual hierarchy to surface urgency and confidence.
- Provide plain-language interpretation alongside metrics.

Success state for dashboards:

A user should know what changed, why it matters, and what to do next in under 10 seconds.

## 14. Empty State Philosophy

Empty states are onboarding moments, not dead ends.

Each empty state should include:

- Why this section is empty.
- What the user should do next.
- A clear primary action.
- Optional secondary guidance.

Tone:

- Encouraging and direct.
- Professional and respectful.
- Never sarcastic or overly playful.

## Why This Will Feel Premium (Not Template-Like)

This design language avoids the most common AI-template traps:

- It rejects generic blue-purple "AI" palettes in favor of a distinct brand color architecture.
- It prioritizes editorial typography and spacing discipline over trend-heavy decoration.
- It positions AI as credible coaching infrastructure, not a spectacle.
- It balances warmth and authority, which fits education and career outcomes.
- It creates a system that can scale from marketing to product UI without visual drift.

The result is a brand and interface language that feels intentional, trustworthy, and mature enough for institutional adoption while still feeling modern and aspirational for students.
